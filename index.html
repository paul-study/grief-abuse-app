Understanding the Cycle of Abuse

Recognizing patterns of abuse is crucial for breaking free. The cycle of abuse typically follows predictable stages.

1. Tension Building

Stress and tension accumulate. Minor incidents occur, communication breaks down, and the victim may feel like they're "walking on eggshells."

  • Increased anxiety and fear
  • Attempts to placate the abuser
  • Feeling powerless and trapped
  • Hyper-vigilance to mood changes

2. Acute Incident

The explosion of violence - physical, emotional, verbal, or sexual abuse occurs. This is the shortest but most destructive phase.

  • Physical or emotional outbursts
  • Intense fear and trauma
  • Unpredictable behavior
  • Victim feels completely powerless

3. Reconciliation (Honeymoon)

The abuser apologizes, shows remorse, makes promises to change, and may shower the victim with affection and gifts.

  • Apologies and promises
  • Expressions of love
  • Blaming external factors
  • Victim feels hope for change

4. Calm Period

A period of relative peace where the abuse may seem to have stopped. The abuser may act as if the abuse never happened.

  • Temporary peace
  • Normal relationship activities
  • Victim's hope strengthens
  • Cycle preparation to repeat

Breaking the Cycle

Understanding these patterns is the first step. The cycle tends to repeat and often escalates over time. If you recognize these patterns, reach out for help. You deserve safety and respect.

Physical Abuse

Hitting, kicking, pushing, restraining, or any unwanted physical contact that causes harm or fear.

Emotional/Psychological Abuse

Manipulation, gaslighting, intimidation, humiliation, isolation from friends and family, or controlling behavior.

Verbal Abuse

Name-calling, yelling, threatening, blaming, or using words to control, hurt, or demean.

Financial Abuse

Controlling finances, preventing employment, or creating financial dependency.

Sexual Abuse

Any non-consensual sexual activity, coercion, or using sex as a form of control.

Digital Abuse

Monitoring devices, demanding passwords, harassment through technology, or posting harmful content online.

The Five Stages of Grief

Grief is a natural response to loss. Understanding these stages can help you navigate your healing journey. Remember, grief is not linear - you may experience these stages in any order.

1

Denial

"This can't be happening"

Denial helps us survive the initial shock and pain. It's a defense mechanism that allows us to process the loss at our own pace.

What you might experience:

  • Shock and disbelief
  • Numbness or feeling disconnected
  • Avoiding the reality of the loss
  • Difficulty accepting what happened

Coping strategies:

  • Allow yourself time to process
  • Talk to someone you trust
  • Don't rush the process
  • Practice self-compassion
2

Anger

"Why is this happening? It's not fair!"

As reality sets in, anger emerges. You may direct it at others, yourself, or even the person you lost. This is a natural and necessary part of healing.

What you might experience:

  • Frustration and irritability
  • Blaming others or yourself
  • Feeling abandoned or betrayed
  • Questioning "why me?"

Coping strategies:

  • Express your feelings safely
  • Exercise or physical activity
  • Journal your thoughts
  • Seek professional support
3

Bargaining

"If only... I would do anything to change this"

In this stage, we try to negotiate or make deals to reverse or lessen the loss. We focus on "what if" and "if only" statements.

What you might experience:

  • "If only" thoughts
  • Trying to negotiate with a higher power
  • Dwelling on what could have been done differently
  • Feeling guilty about things you did or didn't do

Coping strategies:

  • Acknowledge your thoughts without judgment
  • Practice mindfulness
  • Focus on the present moment
  • Challenge guilt with reality
4

Depression

"I'm too sad to do anything"

As the full weight of the loss becomes real, sadness and despair may set in. This is a natural response, not a sign of weakness or mental illness.

What you might experience:

  • Deep sadness and crying
  • Withdrawal from others
  • Loss of interest in activities
  • Changes in sleep or appetite
  • Feeling overwhelmed

Coping strategies:

  • Maintain connections with loved ones
  • Establish a daily routine
  • Engage in gentle self-care
  • Consider counseling or therapy
5

Acceptance

"I'm learning to live with this"

Acceptance doesn't mean you're "over it" or happy about the loss. It means you've come to terms with the new reality and are learning to move forward.

What you might experience:

  • Finding a new sense of normal
  • More good days than bad
  • Ability to recall memories without intense pain
  • Re-engagement with life
  • Finding meaning in the experience

Moving forward:

  • Honor your journey and progress
  • Continue self-care practices
  • Help others going through similar experiences
  • Create new routines and connections

Important Reminders About Grief

  • Grief is not linear: You may move back and forth between stages, and that's completely normal.
  • Everyone grieves differently: There's no "right" way to grieve or timeline you must follow.
  • Grief can resurface: Anniversaries, holidays, or unexpected triggers may bring back intense feelings.
  • Seeking help is strength: Professional support can provide valuable tools and perspective.

Safety Planning

If you're in an abusive relationship, it's important to have a safety plan. This might include:

  • Keeping important documents in a safe place outside your home
  • Having a code word to alert trusted friends or family
  • Knowing safe places you can go in an emergency
  • Having emergency numbers saved in your phone under different names
  • Packing an emergency bag with essentials

Contact any of the services above to help create a personalized safety plan.

Browser Safety

If someone may be monitoring your internet use, consider:

  • Using a device they don't have access to (friend's phone, library computer)
  • Using private/incognito browsing mode
  • Clearing your browser history after visiting this site
  • Having a quick exit strategy ready (keep another tab open)
